According to Bourdieu, judgements of taste express a class-based habitus, and are one of the defining elements of social identity.

“Nothing more clearly affirms one’s ‘class’, nothing more infallibly classifies, than tastes in music…there is no more ‘classificatory’ practice than concert-going, or playing a ‘noble’ instrument.”
(Bourdieu, 1979, Distinction, p. 18).

kitsch

Some theories about the origin of “kitsch”

  • word emerged in the art markets of Munich in the 1860s and 1870s, describing cheap, popular, and marketable pictures and sketches
  • From the dialect German word, kitschen - to smear
  • From the German verb verkitschen - to cheapen
  • A mispronunciation of the English word sketch, or an inversion of the French chic (fashionable)
